About Me

I’m Juan Gudino, a mixed methods researcher and PhD student in Health Services at the University of Washington. I belive that people's stories are more than just data points. Especially the ones we’re not always trained to listen to in public health. My research honors the strengths of Latino and immigrant communities—shaped by my own upbringing in rural Iowa.

My intellectual identity is rooted in epistemic justice, art, and storytelling. I approach health as a live historical narrative shaped by a mixture of systems. Health outcomes are snapshots to the realities that people face simply trying to thrive. I try to understand these snapshots under a population-health lens in epidemiology and health services methodology.

My dissertation examines how psychosocial strengths—like social support, coping strategies, social support, and social networks—can promote mental well-being among Latina immigrant women, even amid adverse structures to health. I’m also a researcher at Fred Hutch Cancer Center, where I help support the implementation of a Sequential Multiple Assignment Randomized Trial focused on weight loss with Latina breast cancer survivors. Across projects, I work with interdisclipinary teams at the intersection of statistical evaluation and storytelling.

More broadly, I see public health as a space where we must document risk without reducing communities to the single dimension of harm. I believe our methodologies should be critically interrogate not only what we measure, but how, and why. I’m passionate about imagining research as a collaborative tool for justice, healing, and transformation.

In my free time, I’m an avid recreational runner, a dog dad to Gaius, and a karaoke local (former collegiate a cappella baritone). I’m also trying to learn chess—and can often be found toggling between research papers, books, and Catan strategy. I’m a goofy guy who finds joy in stories, music, art, and basketball.
